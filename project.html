The Economics of Crime

Project Aims

The seminal work of Gary Becker in 1968 introduced the world to the Economics of Crime. Becker's theory of crime modelled an economically rational criminal. One who is deterred through harsher sanctions and better legal alternatives in society.

My project will attempt to visualise Becker's predictions on crime at the aggregate level. Using US and UK data, I will explore an array of economic measures which characterise better legal alternatives to crime. I will also explore legislative change to the US penal system, which characterises the gain to criminal activity.

I acknowledge inferring causality from correlations is often clouded by issues of reverse causility and other omitted variables.

Discussion

Reducing the gain from crime reduces criminal activity. Prison produces a deterrent effect on criminals. I have attempted to visualise a deterrent effect in action. In 1994, California introduced the Three-Strikes Law. Its implementation introduced a life sentence for an array of violent crimes upon the third strike. Taking its implementation as an event study, a deterrent effect is seen in California with violent crime falling. I chose to concatenate neighbouring states of Nevada and Arizona into the Vega specification, an interesting unintended side-effect discussed by Iyengar (2008) presents. After Three-Strikes, violent crime in neighbouring states increased, implying that criminals may have mitgrated away from California to commit crimes.

As expected, Poverty Rate is positively correlated with Crime Rate, albeit an R-squared of 0.24 is quite weak. Using 2021 data, poverty data is defined by and taken from the U.S Census Bureau and is adjusted each year with CPI. In line with theory, the gain from crime for those in poverty may outweigh the legal alternatives presented in society.

As explored above using US data, poverty is positively correlated with crime. Focusing on the lowest earners in society, I decided to use UK data to investigate how employment affects crime. Utilising the DWP's data tool Stat-Xplore I found data on Universal Credit Claimants in England segmented by employment status whilst claiming and by Local Authority. I downloaded data in excel and manually calculated the ratio of employed claimants per Local Authority. In line with theory, the correlation shows a negative relationship. Employment reduces crime through the mechanisms of supplementary income on top of welfare (increasing legal alternatives to crime) whilst also incapacitating people from committing crime through time constraints of employment.

I have concatenated similar sized Bristol and Liverpool together for comparison. Both cities' criminal activity is dominated by violent crime, however in other categories they differ. The second chart compares the cities by economic indicator of choice. Bristol performs better across all indicators which can be interpreted as reflecting in the overall lower number of crimes.

Conclusion

To conclude, my visualisations have presented support for Becker's theory on crime at the aggregate level. I acknowledge that exploring the drivers of crime rigourously, requires careful econometric design. There are many alternate variables which can drive criminal activity. Many drivers lie outside the field of economics entirely, requiring further invesitgation.
